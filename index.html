iRobot Roomba j9+ / Combo j9+

Autonomous Floor Cleaning · $799–$1,399 · Mass Market

iRobot pioneered consumer robot vacuums and remains the most recognized brand, though it faces intensifying competition from Roborock and Ecovacs on price-performance and post-acquisition uncertainty following Amazon's failed $1.4B deal. Robot vacuum sales have grown 28% year-over-year, with the broader household robot market projected to reach $107B by 2034.1

Jobs to Be Done

The core job is "keep my floors clean without spending my time on it." But the higher-order job — especially for pet owners and dual-income households — is autonomy from the chore itself. Users don't want to manage the robot; they want to forget it exists between maintenance intervals.

What's Working

  • PrecisionVision obstacle avoidance — the camera-based system reliably detects cables, pet bowls, and shoes. The P.O.O.P. guarantee (Pet Owner Official Promise) is a clever trust-building mechanism for the pet-owner segment.2
  • Auto-empty dock — extends the interval between human intervention to roughly 60 days.2 This is a Kano "performance" feature that directly maps to the autonomy JTBD.
  • Combo j9+ mechanical mop arm — retracts the mop pad onto the top of the robot when transitioning to carpet, solving a real category pain point that lift-only competitors can't fully address with thick rugs.3

What I'd Change

  • Onboarding time-to-value is too long. The j9+ takes 3–5 mapping runs before rooms are accurately named — competitors complete mapping after a single run.3 BBB and Trustpilot complaints cluster heavily around Wi-Fi pairing failures and app connectivity.4,5 This is a first-run experience problem: if the first 20 minutes are frustrating, you lose trust before the product demonstrates its core value. A/B test opportunity: guided "first clean" wizard vs. current hands-off approach.
  • Error recovery messaging doesn't help users fix the environment. When stuck, the app says "Roomba is stuck" with no spatial context — no map position, no camera snapshot, no suggested fix.3 I'd add visual context to error alerts, similar to how Tesla surfaces what the car's cameras see.
  • Mop reliability is a retention risk. The mechanical arm occasionally catches on rug corners,3 and recent BBB complaints reveal a recurring Error 517 in newer mopping models that iRobot has acknowledged as known.4 When the hardware fix (replacement seals) fails repeatedly, users lose confidence in the product category itself.5

Safety Considerations

Physical risk is low (floor-level, low mass), but the product operates unsupervised around children and pets. The larger safety concern is data: the onboard camera captures detailed home layouts, and iRobot's privacy posture after the failed Amazon acquisition remains unclear to users.

Amazon Astro

Mobile Home Robot · $1,599 · Invite-Only

Astro is a mobile Alexa device with a periscope camera, positioned for home security and companionship. Launched in 2021 via invite-only Day 1 Editions, it remains limited in availability — Bloomberg reports Amazon shipped only a few hundred units in the first six months.6 Amazon has integrated Astro with Ring for security patrols — but internal developers have flagged the combination as a potential privacy concern.7

Jobs to Be Done

The surface-level JTBD is "monitor my home when I'm away." The deeper job is peace of mind — knowing your family, pets, and property are safe without active attention. For elderly care and accessibility use cases, the JTBD shifts to "help me maintain independence at home" — a higher-stakes, more emotionally resonant job that most reviews overlook.

What's Working

  • Patrol mode is genuinely differentiated. Sending Astro to investigate a noise or check a room while away is functionally distinct from static cameras. Ring integration makes this feel like part of a system, not a standalone novelty.
  • Personality design builds emotional trust. The display "eyes" use proportions that mimic infant features — a well-studied social robotics pattern for building attachment.8 Trust is the prerequisite for adoption in any home robot.
  • Accessibility use cases have strongest PMF. Tasks that seem trivial to able-bodied users — retrieving items, answering the doorbell, medication reminders — are genuinely valuable to elderly and disabled users.9 This is where Astro's product-market fit is strongest.

What I'd Change

  • Privacy controls must be a first-run experience, not a settings menu. Astro carries always-on cameras and microphones through your home. Mozilla's Privacy Not Included review found data collection practices opaque.10 I'd redesign onboarding to surface privacy as an explicit, non-skippable step with clear visual language showing what data goes where.
  • A/B test: proactive vs. reactive check-ins. Does "I noticed unusual movement in the kitchen" increase or decrease trust? I'd segment by user archetype (security-first vs. companion-first) and test notification cadences, measuring both subjective trust scores and feature engagement.
  • Expand the task library or reposition. Astro can't do enough to justify $1,599 as a utility device — multiple reviewers have described it as essentially a mobile Echo Show.9 Amazon needs to either add physical manipulation capabilities or lean fully into the accessibility and eldercare positioning where the existing feature set has actual PMF.

Safety Considerations

Astro represents the convergence of mobile robotics and home surveillance — the most sensitive category combination. Ring operators can remotely control Astro to investigate alerts, meaning a third-party human can move a camera through your home.11 Security experts at F-Secure and McAfee have warned that Amazon's data monetization model creates inherent tension with the granularity of data Astro collects.12 For any home robot beta program, this underscores the importance of building privacy as a product pillar, not a compliance checkbox.

Figure 02 / Figure 03

Humanoid Robot · $30K–$150K (est.) · Industrial Pilot Deployments

Figure AI has moved from demos to commercial deployment: Figure 02 operates at BMW's South Carolina plant,13 and Figure 03 was named a TIME Best Invention of 2025 for home-oriented capabilities.14 The company raised $675M at a $2.6B valuation with backing from Microsoft, OpenAI, and NVIDIA.15 But the gap between demo polish and deployment reliability remains the central question — and the one most relevant to Sunday's own trajectory.

Jobs to Be Done

In industrial contexts: "perform repetitive, physically demanding tasks in human-designed spaces without reconfiguring the workspace." The humanoid form factor is the differentiator — it operates in spaces built for humans. For the eventual home context, the JTBD mirrors Sunday Memo's, but home deployment is 2–4 years away at minimum.16

What's Working

  • Real commercial deployment. BMW is a paying customer. Figure 02 operates in the body shop performing precision part insertion13 — the most credible proof point in the humanoid category.
  • OpenAI integration lowers the programming barrier. The Helix model enables verbal commands translated into physical action sequences, dramatically reducing setup complexity compared to traditional industrial robots.15
  • Hardware iteration speed. 16-DoF hands can lift 25 kg and manipulate tools.15 Battery life of 2–5 hours approaches the useful minimum for factory work, though it still falls short of the 8-hour shift standard.17

What I'd Change

  • The beta feedback loop is underspecified. For a product this early, the data pipeline from deployment to improvement is everything. How are BMW workers reporting failures? How do edge cases route back to training? Sunday's Skill Capture Glove ($200 vs. $20K teleoperation rigs) offers a more scalable data collection model.18 I'd prioritize structured incident reporting infrastructure before scaling deployments.
  • Battery life is a binding constraint. As IEEE Spectrum documented in October 2025, bipedal walking consumes enormous energy just maintaining balance. In practice, Agility's Digit operates in only 30-minute intervals at Amazon warehouses; Figure 02 manages 2–3 hours of active runtime.17 Deployment will remain limited to short-cycle tasks until battery density improves.
  • Safety standards don't exist yet. No ISO standard covers dynamically balancing legged robots in human workspaces.17 Traditional industrial robots operate behind safety cages governed by ISO 10218 — humanoids walk through the same spaces as workers. Liability questions (manufacturer vs. deployer vs. AI provider) are unresolved. Any company deploying humanoids should invest in proactive safety framework development alongside deployment, not after incidents.

Sunday Memo Comparison

Sunday's Memo makes an instructive design contrast. By choosing a wheeled base over bipedal locomotion, Memo eliminates the balance problem — it has passive stability, meaning you can cut power and it won't fall.19 This is a safety-first architectural decision that also solves the battery problem (wheels are far more energy-efficient). The tradeoff is reduced mobility (no stairs), but for kitchens, dining rooms, and laundry, the wheeled approach is arguably the right choice for homes. Sunday has trained Memo on approximately 10 million real-world household episodes from over 500 homes using its Skill Capture Glove system.20

Framework Note: Why JTBD Over Feature Lists

These teardowns use the Jobs to Be Done framework rather than traditional feature comparison because consumer robotics is a category where the user's underlying motivation — not the spec sheet — determines adoption. A robot vacuum with better suction doesn't win if the first-run experience destroys trust. A humanoid that can fold clothes doesn't win if families feel surveilled. Understanding the job helps identify which features are table stakes (Kano "must-haves"), which drive satisfaction (performance features), and which create delight — and which are missing entirely.

Study Design

Semi-structured interviews (45–60 min each) with 6 participants across different household types. Recruitment screened for diversity across household composition, tech comfort level, and prior smart-home experience. Interview guide structured around four modules: current chore delegation, trust thresholds, privacy mental models, and safety expectations. All research operations — participant tracker, session notes, interview guide, and thematic analysis — managed in Notion.

Notion: Interview Guide

Semi-Structured Protocol

Module 1 Current Chore Delegation +

Goal: Understand existing patterns of task delegation and tolerance for imperfect execution.

  • Walk me through your typical evening routine after dinner. What gets cleaned up, and who does it?
  • Have you ever hired someone to help with household tasks? What did you delegate first, and what did you keep?
  • When a chore gets done imperfectly — say, dishes not fully clean — how do you react? Does it matter who did it?
  • If you could permanently remove one recurring household task, what would it be and why?
Module 2 Trust Thresholds +

Goal: Map the spectrum from eager delegation to firm refusal — and identify what shifts the threshold.

  • Imagine a robot could handle [their most-hated chore]. What would it need to do in the first week for you to trust it?
  • What's a task you'd never let a robot do, even if it could? Why?
  • How many times would the robot need to succeed before you'd stop watching it? How many failures before you'd unplug it?
  • Would you trust it more if you could see a live camera feed of what it's doing, or does that feel like more surveillance?
Module 3 Privacy Mental Models +

Goal: Surface how people conceptualize data collection by a physical device in their home — distinct from phone/app privacy.

  • This robot would have cameras and microphones to navigate. How does that make you feel compared to, say, your phone listening?
  • Would you want to know exactly what data the robot collects? Or would you prefer a simple "it's private" assurance?
  • If the company said the data stays on the robot and never goes to the cloud — does that change your comfort level?
  • Who in your household would you want to control the privacy settings? Should everyone have equal access?
Module 4 Safety Expectations +

Goal: Understand how non-technical users define "safety" in the context of a physical robot in their home.

  • When I say "robot safety," what's the first thing that comes to mind?
  • If the robot knocked over a glass and broke it, how would you want to be notified? How quickly?
  • [For parents] What would the robot need to do — or not do — for you to leave it running while your child is in the room?
  • [For pet owners] How do you think your pet would react? What would you need to see before you felt comfortable?
  • If something went wrong, how would you want to report it — in the app, by calling someone, or should the robot tell you itself?

Notion: Affinity Map

Emergent Themes

Themes clustered from interviews using affinity mapping. Each theme includes prevalence (how many participants surfaced it), representative quotes, and product implications.

6/6

Graduated Trust

Every participant described a "probation period" — a phase where they'd actively supervise the robot before trusting it to operate unsupervised. The length varied (1 week to 2 months) and correlated with whether children were present.

P1 "I'd watch it do dishes maybe 10 times. If it doesn't chip anything, okay, it can do its thing."
P5 "First month, it only runs when the baby is napping and I'm in the room."
Product implication: Onboarding should include an explicit "supervised mode" phase with a visible success counter — reframing probation as a designed feature, not user anxiety.
5/6

Physical > Digital Privacy Concern

Five participants distinguished between phone-based data collection (accepted, normalized) and a physical robot with cameras (viscerally uncomfortable). The mobility of the device was the key differentiator — "it can go places I don't expect."

P4 "My phone stays in my pocket. This thing walks into my bedroom."
P6 "It's not the camera that bothers me, it's that it moves. A Nest cam sits in one spot."
Product implication: Privacy onboarding needs to address mobility specifically — show the user a map of where the robot will and won't go. "Out-of-bounds" zones should be a first-run feature, not a settings-menu discovery.
4/6

The Kill Switch Expectation

Four participants — all in households with children or pets — independently described wanting a physical, hardware-level stop mechanism. App-based controls were seen as insufficient because "what if the app crashes?" or "what if my phone is in the other room?"

P5 "A big red button. On the robot itself. I'm not opening an app while my toddler is crying."
P3 "If the dog freaks out, I need to stop it in two seconds, not twenty."
Product implication: Physical stop button is a Kano "must-have" for family households. Marketing should emphasize this as a designed safety feature.
4/6

Task Hierarchy: Start Low-Stakes

Participants consistently described a preferred delegation order — starting with low-stakes, low-emotional-attachment tasks and graduating to higher-stakes ones. Dishes and tidying were universally "first tasks." Laundry was more contested (fear of damage to clothing). Childcare-adjacent tasks were firm boundaries.

P2 "Start with the stuff I don't care about. If it folds my gym socks wrong, who cares. My work shirts? Different story."
P1 "Dishes, yes. Tidying, sure. Anything near the kids' stuff — not until I really trust it."
Product implication: Beta programs should launch with the lowest-stakes skills first and sequence new skill rollouts based on user trust level, not engineering readiness.
3/6

Incident Reporting: Robot Should Speak First

Three participants preferred the robot itself to proactively report incidents ("I dropped a glass") rather than relying on the user to discover and report the problem. This was tied to trust as a robot that self-reports failures felt more trustworthy than one that hides them.

P2 "If it breaks something and tells me, that's fine. If I find broken glass and it didn't say anything? It's going back."
P4 "Honesty matters, even from a machine."
Product implication: Proactive incident alerts are a trust-building mechanism. A/B test: robot-initiated disclosure vs. passive logging.
Hypothesis: proactive reporting increases NPS even when the incident itself is negative.
3/6

Multi-User Access Anxiety

In shared households (roommates, multi-generational), participants raised concerns about who controls the robot, who sees its data, and whose preferences take priority. This was especially acute for the roommate household and the multi-generational home.

P6 "If my roommate can see the robot's activity log, they can see when I come and go. That's surveillance."
P4 "My mom would want completely different settings than me. Who wins?"
Product implication: Multi-user profiles with per-user privacy controls are essential for shared households. Early consumer screening should assess household dynamics. A single enthusiast in a skeptical household may generate friction and not usable data.

Deliverable: Personas

Trust Spectrum

Three user archetypes mapped to a trust spectrum — from eager delegation to conditional acceptance to firm resistance. Each persona includes adoption triggers, deal-breakers, and beta program fit assessment.

Archetype A

The Eager Optimizer

Tech-forward individual who already has a smart home ecosystem. Views a home robot as the next logical upgrade. Willing to tolerate imperfect execution in exchange for time savings. Trust is earned quickly through functionality, not gradually through observation.

Represented by: P2, P6
Adoption trigger: "It works out of the box without me configuring everything."
Deal-breaker: Complex setup, frequent manual intervention, poor app UX.
Beta fit: High — will generate rich feedback on edge cases and feature requests. Tolerant of early-stage bugs.
Archetype B

The Cautious Parent

Family-oriented user whose trust develops gradually through observed reliability. Physical safety of children and pets is the primary lens. Will impose a supervised probation period. Wants visible, hardware-level control. Privacy matters but is secondary to physical safety.

Represented by: P1, P3, P5
Adoption trigger: "I watched it work 20 times and nothing bad happened."
Deal-breaker: Any incident involving a child or pet, even minor. No physical stop mechanism.
Beta fit: Medium — critical for safety testing and family-context data, but may have lower tolerance for failure. Needs extra onboarding support.
Archetype C

The Privacy-First Skeptic

User who frames the robot primarily as a surveillance device that happens to do chores. Trust is blocked by data concerns, not functionality concerns. Wants full transparency on what data is collected, where it goes, and who accesses it. Often the secondary user in the household (not the person who bought it).

Represented by: P4, P6 (partial)
Adoption trigger: "I can see exactly what it knows about me and delete it."
Deal-breaker: Cloud data transmission, opaque privacy policy, no per-user access controls.
Beta fit: Lower — may resist participation, but including 1–2 skeptics in a beta is essential for stress-testing privacy features and identifying consent UX gaps.

A/B Testing Hypotheses

The study identifies concrete testable hypotheses for home robotics deployment programs. Onboarding: guided walkthrough vs. self-exploration (metric: time-to-first-successful-task and 7-day retention).
Privacy consent: granular opt-in vs. simplified defaults (metric: completion rate and post-onboarding trust survey score).
Feedback cadence: daily in-app prompt vs. weekly summary digest (metric: response rate and qualitative depth).
Incident reporting: user-initiated in-app report vs. robot-initiated prompt after anomalous behavior (metric: report volume and time-to-report).
